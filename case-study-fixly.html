Back to work Case Study — UI/UX Design

Fixly — cutting a home-service booking flow from 6 taps to 3.

A mobile marketplace connecting homeowners with local service professionals — cleaning, repairs, and maintenance — booked, scheduled, and paid for without the usual friction.

Role
Solo UI/UX Designer
Timeline
4 weeks
Tools
Figma, FigJam
Platform
Mobile (iOS/Android)
The problem

Booking a repair shouldn't feel harder than the repair itself.

Most home-service apps I looked at (and the gaps I noticed using similar apps myself) buried the actual booking action under category browsing, profile comparisons, and a multi-step scheduler — often six or more taps before a user could confirm a time and pay. For a category where people are often already frustrated (a leaking pipe, a broken AC), that friction is the whole problem.

I set out to design a flow where finding a trustworthy professional and locking in a time slot could happen almost as fast as the user could describe what was wrong.

Key decisions

Three choices that shaped the final flow.

Decision 01

Lead with the problem, not the category tree

The home screen opens with "What's going on?" — a short list of the top real-world problems (leak, AC, electrical) rather than an abstract service-category grid, since users searching for help rarely think in the marketplace's own taxonomy.

Decision 02

One screen for "who" and "when"

Professional cards show their next available slot inline; tapping a slot books it directly, collapsing what was a 3-screen sequence in comparable apps into one scrollable list.

Decision 03

Payment is a confirmation, not a form

With address and card saved during onboarding, the final step is a single "Confirm booking — 250 EGP" button rather than a checkout form, mirroring the one-tap-checkout pattern from mature e-commerce apps.

Outcome & reflection

From 6 taps to 3, and a few honest lessons.

The final flow takes a returning user from "What's going on?" to a confirmed booking in three taps: pick the problem, pick a pro's slot, confirm. New users add one extra step for the one-time address/payment setup.

This was a solo, speculative project without access to real users or analytics, so I validated the flow through informal walkthroughs with classmates rather than in-market data.
